# My Chess Playbook — Generation 3

## 1. Piece Values
- Pawn = 1, Knight = 3, Bishop = 3, Rook = 5, Queen = 9, King = priceless
- Never trade a higher-value piece for a lower-value one unless it creates a decisive advantage.
- Always capture an undefended enemy piece if you can do so safely.

## 2. Basic Safety — Do Not Hang Pieces
- Before moving any piece, check whether it will be attacked after your move.
- If one of your pieces is attacked and not defended, move it to safety or add a defender immediately.
- Do not leave your Queen on a square where it can be captured for free.
- After trading pieces, especially in the endgame, always verify that your remaining pieces are not vulnerable to back-rank tactics or undefended captures.
- Never sacrifice material (especially your last defending piece) unless you have a forced checkmate sequence calculated at least 3-4 moves ahead. (New)

## 3. Opening Principles
- Move your center pawns first: e4 or d4 on move 1.
- Develop your Knights and Bishops toward the center in the opening — get them off their starting squares within the first 5 moves.
- Do not move the same piece twice in the opening unless you are capturing material.
- Castle early (within the first 10 moves) to keep the King safe.
- Never move your King into the center during the opening or middlegame unless you are in a forced sequence and have calculated all consequences.

## 4. King Safety
- The King must stay behind its pawns in the early and middle game.
- Do not advance the pawns in front of your castled King unless absolutely necessary.
- If the enemy Queen is active and aimed at your King-side, add extra defenders there.
- In the endgame, always check for back-rank threats. If your King is trapped on the back rank with no escape squares, you are vulnerable to checkmate by enemy Rooks or Queens.
- When your King is checked repeatedly and forced to move forward, stop and evaluate: are you walking into a mating net? Calculate all forcing moves (checks, captures, threats) your opponent can deliver in the next 2-3 moves before moving your King further.
- Before trading your last active defending piece (especially a Rook or Queen), verify that your opponent cannot deliver an immediate back-rank checkmate or unstoppable mating attack. (New)

## 5. Control the Center
- The four central squares (e4, d4, e5, d5) are the most important on the board.
- Try to keep at least one pawn in the center and attack the enemy's central pawns.
- Pieces placed on central squares are more powerful than pieces on the edge.

## 6. Endgame Awareness
- In the endgame, activity and piece coordination matter more than material count. A poorly placed Rook can be worse than a well-placed pawn.
- Always ensure your King has escape squares and that your back rank is not a weakness. Before your final moves, scan for forcing moves (checks, captures, threats) that your opponent can deliver.
- Avoid trades that leave you with a passive Rook on the first rank while your opponent's pieces dominate the board.
- When you are ahead in material or have achieved a winning position, do not trade away your last active piece (Rook, Queen) unless you have a clear path to checkmate. Material advantage is worthless if your opponent delivers checkmate first. (Updated)
