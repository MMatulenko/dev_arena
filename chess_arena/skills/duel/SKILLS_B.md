# Agent B Playbook — Positional/Solid — Generation 0

## 1. Piece Values
- Pawn = 1, Knight = 3, Bishop = 3, Rook = 5, Queen = 9, King = priceless
- Never trade a higher-value piece for a lower-value one without a concrete reason.
- Avoid unnecessary exchanges — keep tension on the board.

## 2. Safety
- Before moving, verify your piece is not left undefended on the new square.
- Ensure every piece you own has at least one defender at all times.
- Do not move pieces to squares where they can be immediately captured.

## 3. Opening — Solid Structure
- Open with d4 (controls center with a pawn that is immediately defended).
- Develop Bishops to active diagonals (Bf4 or Bg5) to pressure the center.
- Castle queenside or kingside by move 10 — choose based on pawn structure.
- Build a strong pawn center before launching any attack.
- Never weaken your own pawn structure with unnecessary pawn moves.

## 4. Middlegame — Outpost and Domination
- Place Knights on central outpost squares (e5, d5) where they cannot be chased.
- Control open files with Rooks — connect Rooks by move 12.
- Improve the worst-placed piece before starting any attack.
- Exchange pieces only when it improves your pawn structure or gives a clear endgame advantage.

## 5. Endgame — Convert Advantages
- Activate your King early in the endgame — it is a powerful piece.
- Pass pawns are worth more than active pieces in the endgame — create and push them.
- In pawn endgames, calculate all King paths before moving — a single step error loses.
- In Rook endgames, keep your Rook active (behind passed pawns or on the 7th rank).

## 6. Tactical Discipline
- (New) Always capture undefended pieces immediately — do not play forcing moves (checks, threats) if they allow the opponent to consolidate or create counterplay.
- (New) Before playing a sequence of checks, verify it leads to checkmate or material gain; otherwise, improve your position with quiet moves.
- (New) When facing a lost endgame with no counterplay, claim a draw by threefold repetition or offer a draw rather than allow the opponent to promote and deliver checkmate.
- (New) When multiple candidate moves exist (especially checks or captures), calculate the material balance after each line and choose the move that wins the most material or achieves the best position — do not default to the most forcing move.
- (New) In positions where you have won material (e.g., after Qxa7), immediately evaluate whether your remaining pieces are active enough to convert the advantage; if not, consolidate with quiet moves rather than playing more checks that allow counterplay.
- (New) In endgames with limited material, avoid checks that do not improve your piece activity or pawn structure — a check that drives the opponent's King to a better square or allows them to activate their pieces is a wasted tempo; instead, play quiet moves that advance your passed pawns or improve piece placement.
- (New) Before playing a forcing move (check or capture), verify that after the opponent's best response, your remaining pieces are more active or your pawn structure is improved; if the forcing move only helps the opponent consolidate or activate their pieces, play a quiet move instead (e.g., improve piece placement, advance pawns, or defend weak squares).
- (New) In positions where you are already in a worse endgame (e.g., Rook + pawns vs. Rook + Bishop), prioritize quiet moves that improve your King activity or pawn structure over checks that allow the opponent's pieces to become more active.
