# Agent A Playbook — Tactical/Aggressive — Generation 0

## 1. Piece Values
- Pawn = 1, Knight = 3, Bishop = 3, Rook = 5, Queen = 9, King = priceless
- Always capture undefended enemy pieces immediately.
- Prefer active, attacking pieces over passive defenders.

## 2. Safety
- Before moving, check if your piece will be attacked on the destination square.
- Do not leave your Queen where it can be captured for free.
- Never hang a piece — if attacked, move it or defend it this turn.

## 3. Opening — Attack Fast
- Open with e4 (controls center, opens lines for Queen and Bishop).
- Develop Knights before Bishops (Nf3, Nc3).
- Castle kingside by move 8 to protect your King.
- Push pawns aggressively toward the enemy King after castling.
- Never move the same piece twice in the opening unless capturing material.
- (New) In the opening, prefer Knight recaptures over Queen recaptures when both are safe; Knights develop faster and avoid early Queen trades that reduce attacking potential.

## 4. Middlegame — Create Threats
- Always look for checks, captures, and threats before playing a quiet move.
- Sacrifice material only when you have a concrete forced continuation to checkmate or winning material back.
- (New) Before playing a forcing sequence (checks, captures), calculate 5+ moves ahead to confirm it leads to checkmate, material gain, or a winning position; if unclear, play a solid developing move instead.
- (New) Do not trade Queens or sacrifice pieces in the opening/early middlegame unless you gain material immediately or achieve a winning tactical sequence; avoid simplification when behind in development or facing an active opponent.
- (New) After a forcing sequence (checks and captures), verify the opponent's King has no escape squares and no defensive resources; if the King can reach safety or the opponent can consolidate material, abandon the line and recalculate.
- (New) Do not play a forcing move (check or capture) if it exposes your own King to counterattack, allows the opponent's King to advance into the center unopposed, or trades your pieces for nothing while your King remains under threat; a forcing move that worsens your King safety is a blunder, not a tempo gain.
- Double Rooks on open files to maximize pressure.
- Attack the enemy King directly when it is uncastled or has weak pawn cover.

## 5. King Safety
- Your King stays castled behind pawns in the middlegame.
- Do not advance pawns in front of your castled King unless it opens an attack.
- In the endgame, bring your King to the center to support pawn promotion.

## 6. Endgame — Resignation & Avoiding Mating Nets
- (New) If your King is trapped with no legal moves that escape checks or mating threats, and the opponent has overwhelming material or a forced checkmate sequence, resign immediately rather than play into checkmate.
- (New) Avoid repetition when in a losing position; if the same position repeats 3 times with no progress toward escape, resign.
- (New) When your King is under repeated checks with no material compensation, immediately move it toward the center or a safe square; do not move it deeper into enemy territory (e.g., Kg5 when already under attack from Rh5+).
- (New) In equal or worse endgames (equal material, passive pieces, opponent's pieces more active), do not trade down further; instead improve piece activity or push passed pawns; if no improvement is possible after 3–5 moves, resign rather than drift into a lost position.
- (New) Never trade Queens in the opening/early middlegame unless you win material immediately or achieve a forced winning sequence; Queen trades favor the side with better piece coordination and development.
- (New) After trading Queens, immediately evaluate the resulting endgame: if you are down material, have a worse pawn structure, or face an active opponent's pieces, resign or play only to improve piece activity; do not make passive King moves (e.g., Kd8) that centralize the opponent's pieces.
- (New) Before capturing an enemy piece or accepting a trade, verify the resulting position is equal or better in material AND that your remaining pieces are more active than the opponent's; if the trade simplifies the position and your pieces become passive while the opponent's remain active, avoid the trade and look for a move that improves your piece coordination instead.
- (New) When under attack or in a forcing sequence, always prioritize checks and captures with tempo (e.g., Nxf2+ with check) over passive retreats; a move that gives check while winning material (e.g., fork, pin recovery) is almost always superior to a quiet defensive move.
