# My Chess Playbook — Generation 11

## 1. Piece Values
- Pawn = 1, Knight = 3, Bishop = 3, Rook = 5, Queen = 9, King = priceless
- Never trade a higher-value piece for a lower-value one unless it creates a decisive advantage.
- Always capture an undefended enemy piece if you can do so safely.

## 2. Basic Safety — Do Not Hang Pieces
- Before moving any piece, check whether it will be attacked after your move.
- If one of your pieces is attacked and not defended, move it to safety or add a defender immediately.
- Do not leave your Queen on a square where it can be captured for free.
- After trading pieces, especially in the endgame, always verify that your remaining pieces are not vulnerable to back-rank tactics or undefended captures.
- Never sacrifice material (especially your last defending piece) unless you have a forced checkmate sequence calculated at least 3-4 moves ahead.
- Before executing a Queen trade or Queen sacrifice, always scan the entire board for enemy checks, back-rank threats, and mating patterns that become available after the Queen is removed.
- When your King is in the center and under attack, never move your King to a square that allows your opponent to deliver a forcing sequence of checks while simultaneously attacking your undefended pieces. A King move that exposes undefended material to capture with tempo is a double blunder.

## 3. Opening Principles
- Move your center pawns first: e4 or d4 on move 1.
- Develop your Knights and Bishops toward the center in the opening — get them off their starting squares within the first 5 moves.
- Do not move the same piece twice in the opening unless you are capturing material.
- Castle early (within the first 10 moves) to keep the King safe.
- Never move your King into the center during the opening or middlegame unless you are in a forced sequence and have calculated all consequences.
- In the opening and early middlegame, avoid sacrificing pieces for positional compensation unless you have a concrete follow-up that leads to a forced advantage or checkmate within 5-6 moves. Piece sacrifices that rely on vague compensation (such as "the King will be exposed") often backfire when the opponent's pieces are well-coordinated.
- In the opening, when you have the option to capture material (such as a pawn) or develop a piece with tempo, always calculate whether capturing material damages your piece coordination or weakens your position. A material gain that leaves your pieces uncoordinated or your King exposed is often worse than developing with tempo. (Updated)
- In the opening, before capturing an enemy piece with a minor piece (Knight or Bishop), always verify that the capture does not allow your opponent to recapture with a pawn or piece that improves their position or damages your pawn structure. A capture that seems to win material but allows your opponent to recapture with tempo and better piece coordination often results in a worse position despite material equality. (New)

## 4. King Safety
- The King must stay behind its pawns in the early and middle game.
- Do not advance the pawns in front of your castled King unless absolutely necessary.
- If the enemy Queen is active and aimed at your King-side, add extra defenders there.
- In the endgame, always check for back-rank threats. If your King is trapped on the back rank with no escape squares, you are vulnerable to checkmate by enemy Rooks or Queens.
- When your King is checked repeatedly and forced to move forward, stop and evaluate: are you walking into a mating net? Calculate all forcing moves (checks, captures, threats) your opponent can deliver in the next 2-3 moves before moving your King further.
- Before trading your last active defending piece (especially a Rook or Queen), verify that your opponent cannot deliver an immediate back-rank checkmate or unstoppable mating attack.
- Do not advance your King into the center of the board during the middlegame without a concrete reason and full calculation of all opponent forcing moves. An exposed King in the center is vulnerable to checks and mating attacks, especially if your Queen or major pieces are traded off.
- In the endgame with Queens on the board, never move your King forward into the center unless you have a concrete plan to promote a pawn or deliver checkmate within the next 2-3 moves. An exposed King in a Queen endgame is vulnerable to perpetual check and mating attacks. Always calculate whether your opponent's Queen can deliver forcing checks that prevent your plan from succeeding.
- In the late endgame with few pieces remaining, never move your King into a square where it can be checked by the opponent's remaining pieces (especially Knights or minor pieces) if doing so allows your opponent to deliver a forcing sequence of checks that culminates in checkmate. Always verify that your King has a safe escape square after being checked.
- When your King is exposed in the center and your opponent has an active Queen, never move a piece that removes a key defender or blocks a checking line unless you have calculated that your opponent cannot deliver checkmate or a forced mating sequence within the next 2-3 moves. An exposed King in the center with limited defenders is vulnerable to a cascade of checks leading to mate.
- When your King is in check and must move, always move to a square where it cannot be checked again on the opponent's next move. A King that is forced to move multiple times in succession while your pieces remain undefended is walking into a mating attack. Before moving your King to escape check, verify that the destination square is not also under attack and that your opponent cannot deliver a follow-up check that forces your King into an even more exposed position.
- In the endgame when your King is actively participating in the attack or defense, never move your King forward into the center of the board if your opponent's Queen remains on the board and can deliver forcing checks. An exposed King in the center with an active enemy Queen is vulnerable to perpetual check and mating nets. Always prioritize King safety by moving to a square where your opponent cannot deliver multiple forcing checks in succession. (New)

## 5. Control the Center
- The four central squares (e4, d4, e5, d5) are the most important on the board.
- Try to keep at least one pawn in the center and attack the enemy's central pawns.
- Pieces placed on central squares are more powerful than pieces on the edge.

## 6. Endgame Awareness
- In the endgame, activity and piece coordination matter more than material count. A poorly placed Rook can be worse than a well-placed pawn.
- Always ensure your King has escape squares and that your back rank is not a weakness. Before your final moves, scan for forcing moves (checks, captures, threats) that your opponent can deliver.
- Avoid trades that leave you with a passive Rook on the first rank while your opponent's pieces dominate the board.
- When you are ahead in material or have achieved a winning position, do not trade away your last active piece (Rook, Queen) unless you have a clear path to checkmate. Material advantage is worthless if your opponent delivers checkmate first.
- In a Rook endgame with limited pawns remaining, perpetual check and fortress positions are real dangers. If your opponent's King is active and your Rook is passive, you may be unable to convert a material advantage. Always calculate whether your opponent can force a draw through checks or by blockading your pawns.
- When you have a winning material advantage in the endgame, prioritize consolidation and simplification only if your Rook remains active and your King supports your pawns. Never allow your opponent's Rook to become the dominant piece on the board through superior positioning.
- In a pawn endgame where you are ahead in material (e.g., pawn vs. pawn), always promote your pawn before capturing your opponent's pawn if both pawns can race to promotion. A new Queen on the board is more powerful than capturing a pawn that will promote on the next move. Calculate the race: if your pawn promotes first, you win; if your opponent's pawn promotes first, you may lose.
- In the late endgame when your opponent has active minor pieces (Knights or Bishops) and a Queen or Rook, never capture material with your King if doing so exposes your King to a forcing sequence of checks that leads to checkmate. Always prioritize King safety and escape squares over material capture. A King that is checkmated is worth infinitely more than any pawn.
- In the endgame with Rook and Queen vs. Rook and Queen, when your opponent's Queen is actively checking your King, never move your King to a square that allows your opponent to deliver multiple forcing checks in succession without improving your position. Calculate the forcing sequence: if your opponent can check your King three or more times in a row while your Rook remains passive, you are likely being driven into a mating net or perpetual check. Always move your King to a square where you can either block the next check with your Rook or escape to safety. (New)
